### Post 2: The Deadline Moved — That's Not a Reason to Wait

The FSMA 204 compliance deadline just moved to January 2028.

I've already seen the reaction: "Great, we have more time." And technically, you do. But here's what that extra time doesn't change:

Your products are either on the FDA's Food Traceability List or they aren't. If they are, the requirements are the same whether the deadline is 2026 or 2028. The KDEs you need to capture, the CTEs you need to document, the 24-hour response window — none of that got easier.

What actually happened is the FDA gave companies more runway to build systems that work. Not more runway to keep ignoring the problem.

The companies that use this time to set up traceability now — even imperfectly — will be in a completely different position than those who scramble in Q4 2027.

### Post 3: What a 24-Hour FDA Request Actually Looks Like

Imagine this: FDA contacts you at 2pm on a Tuesday. They need full traceability records for a specific lot of romaine lettuce — every CTE, every KDE, from your receiving dock back to the grower. You have 24 hours.

Now ask yourself:

Where is that data right now? Is it in one system or scattered across three? Can your team pull it without the one person who "knows where everything is"? Could you do it if that request came on a Friday afternoon?

Most companies I talk to are honest about the answer: they'd struggle. Not because they don't care about food safety — they do — but because their systems weren't designed for this kind of real-time traceability.

That's not a failure. It's just reality. FSMA 204 is asking companies to operate at a level most have never needed before.

### Post 6: Supplier Data Is Your Biggest Risk

You can have the best internal traceability system in the world and still fail a compliance audit. The reason? Supplier data.

FSMA 204 requires traceability across the entire supply chain. That means the data your suppliers send you — lot numbers, harvest dates, ship-to locations — has to be accurate, complete, and in a format you can actually use.

Here's what I see constantly: suppliers sending CSVs with missing fields, inconsistent date formats, lot numbers that don't match purchase orders. It's not malicious. It's just that nobody told them exactly what format you need, and there's no system enforcing it.

The fix isn't yelling at your suppliers. It's giving them a clear spec and validating incoming data automatically before it enters your system.
